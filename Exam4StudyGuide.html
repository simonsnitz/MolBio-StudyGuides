RNA Motility and Decay

* All RNAs that are exported to the cytoplasm go through the nuclear pore complex, but different types of RNAs use different pathways. A general export receptor interacts with the RNA either directly or through adapter proteins.

* The directionality for exportin-mediated export is provided by cycles of GTP binding and hydrolysis by the Ran protein. The mRNA export pathway may use cycles of phosphorylation of an adapter SR protein to achieve directionality. A DEAD-box ATPase may also give directionality.

* It is becoming apparent that many RNAs are further localized within the cytoplasm. This may have profound effects on the coordination and regulation of gene expression.

* RNA decay is critical for gene regulation and plays other roles. The basic process of RNA decay is: deadenylation followed either by 1) continued exonuclease digestion 3’ to 5’ by the exosome, or 2) decapping. If decapped, the mRNA is typically degraded 5’ to 3’ by Xrn1.

Ribosome & Initiation

* The ribosome consists of two subunits, the large and small subunits, which are composed of RNA and protein. The large subunit includes the peptidyl transferase center and the small subunit decodes mRNA.

* Prokaryotic and eukaryotic translation initiation have some common features. A charged initiator tRNA forms a complex with the small subunit that recognizes a start codon, followed by joining of the large subunit, GTP hydrolysis, and ultimately initiation.

* Eukaryotic initiation has unique features; most is cap-dependent and occurs by scanning, the mRNA circularizes, and there are more factors. Some mRNAs (especially viral) use IRES elements.

* Prokaryotes and eukaryotes have varied mechanisms for control of translation. These mechanisms commonly use RNA-RNA interactions and RNA structures.

Translation Process

* All organisms use essentially the same genetic code; three nucleotides comprise a codon, which specifies an amino acid or signals termination.

* Translation elongation consists of cycles of 1) multi-step binding of aminoacyl tRNA in a ternary complex with EF-Tu and GTP; 2) peptidyl transfer, catalyzed by the 50S subunit; and 3) translocation, which is greatly facilitated by EF-G and GTP.

* Termination is mediated by the appropriate stop codons and release factors, which recognize them. The release factors bind in the A-site and may contribute directly to hydrolysis to liberate the peptide chain.

* Complex systems are present to ensure the degradation of damaged mRNAs that are unable to be translated accurately into protein. These pathways include nonsense-mediated decay (NMD), nonstop decay, and no-go decay.

Regulatory Small RNAs

* Small RNAs, in the forms of siRNAs and miRNAs, play large roles in the regulation of gene expression in eukaryotes. They are important in normal cell metabolism, development, and defense against invaders.

* siRNAs are produced from longer segments of dsRNA by Dicer, assembled into RISC, and targeted to mRNAs with perfect complementarity, giving silencing by cleavage and degradation of the RNA or by formation of heterochromatin

* The pathway for miRNA has many steps in common with that for siRNA. However, miRNAs are processed from hairpin precursors, and they most often have imperfect complementarity with their targets, giving effects on translation and on mRNA decay by standard pathways rather than Ago-mediated cleavage of the mRNA

lincRNAs

* Chromosomes are arranged to allow long-range contacts between regulatory elements and promoters, gene ends, and much longer-range contacts involving multiple genes. Contacts maps have led to the view that chromosomes are arranged in a segmented way.

* Genomic methods have indicated the presence of large numbers of long, intervening non-coding RNAs (lincRNAs). While many of these RNAs are likely to be non-functional, some are known to be functional and many others are likely to be functional as well.

* Some lncRNAs act in cis on DNA adjacent to their transcription sites, while others function in trans to regulate gene expression, functioning in the nucleus or the cytoplasm.

* More functions almost certainly exist and are yet to be discovered.

RNA Export

Nuclear Pore Complex

NPC is a large pore with fibrils pointing towards the cytoplasm and a basket facing the nucleus. Composed of 30 different pore proteins. 8-30 copies per complex.

These NPCs are highly efficient Sorting Machines

Export of tRNA

tRNA binds directly to a karyopherin, which recognize Nuclear Export Signals (NES) and Nuclear Localization Signals (NLS). Most are peptide sequences, but can be RNA

After being processed tRNA binds exportin-t. Binding depends on tRNA structure, so this may be a quality control step

Karyopherins interact with Ran, a GTPase that cycles through GTP binding and hydrolysis to transport things into and out of the nucleus.

Binding of Ran-GTP to exportin-t favors the closed state, which binds tRNA tightly and specifically

GTPase activating protein is cytoplasmic (RanGTP), as well as helper RanBP1/2 proteins.

GDP-GTP exchange factor is nuclear (RanGEF, or RCC1). Therefore, Ran is mostly GTP-bound in the nucleus and mostly GDP-bound in the cytoplasm

Exportin-t binds Ran-GTP, so nuclear binding and export are favored

Adaptor Proteins

Export of microRNAs are analogous to tRNAs, but the receptor is Exportin-5 instead of Exportin-t

Export receptor for snRNAs is CRM1 (or exportin-1), which requires a cap-binding complex and NES-containing adaptor protein.

After export, snRNAs are processed, assembled with Sm proteins, and re-imported.

TAP is the transport factor for metazoan mRNAs

TAP binds to ALY/REF adaptor protein, which is a component of transcription and splicing complexes.

Adaptor proteins are diverse (help export snRNAs, miRNAs, tRNAs, and mRNAs)

SR Proteins

SR Proteins can also function as adaptors (and replace ALY/REF in some cases)

SR protein phosphorylation may confer directionality to export

RNP remodeling by DEAD-box protein may confer directionality to export. DEAD-box protein localizes only to cytoplasmic face

Motor-mediated mRNA Transport

Transport can be mediated by kinesin, processive myosin, or dynein motors

Localization mainly depends on: RNA sequences termed "zipcodes", and RNA-binding proteins

Deadenylation

Deadenylation removes poly-A tail. There are 3 major pathways

(1)Main deadenylation complex in yeast contains two nucleases (Ccr4p and Pop2p).Complex is inhibited by Poly-A binding protein. Ccr4p is highly conserved among eukaryotes

Ccr4p possesses deadenylation activity and is central to the activity of the complex. Isolation of Ccr4p co-purifies with deadenylation activity, which is not dependent on Pop2

(1)PAN is a second deadenylation complex. Its activity becomes significant when Ccr4p is deleted. And it is stimulated by Poly-A binding protein

Initial Poly-A shortening my be coupled to nuclear export

(3) PARN my be the major deadenylase in Mammals (absent in yeast and flies)

PARN complexes may be differentially regulated.
Flux through different mRNP states could alter susceptibility to different deadenylation complexes, allowing control

3-prime to 5-prime Decay

3 to 5-prime exonuclease forms a hexameric ring with three additional subunits that form a cap.
Rrp44(the 10th subunit) is responsible for most of the nucleolytic activity
Activity of exosome depends on RNA helicase protein (Ski2) and ATP

Thought that substrate is fed through the ring channel to Rrp44 for degradation

Decapping

If mRNA is not decayed by previous exosome, it is fed through an alternative decap pathway that starts with decapping

Decapping proteins (Dcp1/2) are highly conserved. Dcp2 is the catalytic subunit
Decapping is achieved by hydrolysis, yielding GDP and 5-prime phosphorylated mRNA, a good substrate for 5-to-3-prime exonuclease

Poly-A tail inhibits Decapping through binding to Pab1p. Either forms a ring (cap and tail) through translation or another binding protein and inhibits decapping enzyme from reaching the cap.

P bodies: processing bodies for RNA within the cytoplasm

5-prime to 3-prime Decay

After decapping (alternative route) final step is 5-to-3-prime decay. This is mediated by the Xrn1 protein, which binds RNA
RNA end is buried deep within the active site, thus "trapping" the RNA
The enzyme recongnizes the 5-prime phosphate and removes one nucleotide at a time.

In vitro Assembly

Hierarchal assembly

16S rRNA and 21 ribosomal proteins form an active 30S subunit

In vitro assembly of bacterial ribosomes is hierarchial
- Primary prinding proteins bind first and are prerequisite for the binding of secondary and tertiary proteins
- Individual domains can fold independently
- Primary binding proteins are domain-specific
- Assembly of tertiary proteins is dependent on previous assembly of multiple domains

Hierarchy of Assembly in vitro follows the temporal order of transcription and emergence of nacent RNA from the RNA polymerase

Assembly is more efficient in vivo

Regulatory Small RNAs

* Small RNAs, in the forms of siRNAs and miRNAs, play large roles in the regulation of gene expression in eukaryotes. They are important in normal cell metabolism, development, and defense against invaders.

* siRNAs are produced from longer segments of dsRNA by Dicer, assembed into RISC, and targeted to mRNAs with perfect complementarity, giving silencing by cleavage and degradation of the RNA or by formation of heterochromatin

* The pathway for miRNA has many steps in common with that for siRNA. However, miRNAs are processed from hairpin precursors, and they most often have imperfect complementarity with their targets, giving effects on translation and on mRNA decay by standard pathways rather than Ago-mediated cleavage of the mRNA

History

Some flowers have a purple and white color. Instead of adding purple pigment, some cells produce transgenes that suppress the purple pigment production

Injection of dsRNA in worms shown to cause destruction of specific mRNA
- Injected silencing dsRNA into gonads
- dsRNA had to include exons, introns and promoter didnt work
- Effect was incredibly potent and even spead to other cells within the worm
- Termed RNA interference. Very useful as a tool

dsDNA is processed to small fragments of well-defined length
- Used a system from fly embryo lysate
- Added dsRNA corresponding to luciferase gene
- Fragments were the same length regardless of which strand was labeled
- production of fragments did not require the luciferase mRNA, suggesting that the processing is upsteam of effect on mRNA

Assembly of RISC requires additional proteins
- Processing of dsRNAs into RISC requires accessory proteins: R2D2 in flys forms complex with dicer
- RISC loading complex includes Dicer, R2D2 and possibly other proteins
- Ago2 cleaves the passenger strand, leading to its ejection

Removal of Passenger Strand is Accelerated by C3PO
- C3PO enhances mRNA cleavage by Ago 2 programmed with dsRNA
- Complex of two proteins, Translin and Trax, both of which are required for activity
- Trax hase RNase activity, which is required for promotion of RISC complex
- Suggests that RNase activity is used to remove passenger strand to activate RISC
- Same activity may also remove cleaved mRNA in subsequent functional cycles

Argonaute: Central component of the RISC complex
- One strand of the dsRNA produced by Dicer is retained in the RISC complex in association with argonaute
- in structure without mRNA, guide strand nucleotides 2-6 have bases exposed and available for base pairing

miRNA & its Mechanism

History

GeneA was known to repress GeneB, but required the 3'UTR of GeneB to repress it

Subsequenct analysis showed that GeneA produced a non-coding RNA with complementarity to the critical region in GeneB

Repression in L2 developmental stage was shown to be independent of degradation of the mRNA or even changes in polysome profile
- Now known to be at least 3000 miRNAs in humans

miRNA Biogenesis and RISC assembly

Transcription is by RNA pol II, and transcripts are capped and polyadenylated
- Processed from an imperfect hairpin within transcript, called pri-miRNA

In plants, both processing steps to produce short dsRNA are by Dcl1 in nucleus
- In animals, hairpin is excised by Drosha or by splicing in nucleus to produce pre-miRNA

Then Dicer performs final processing step in the cytoplasm

Extent of Complementarity Determines Whether miRNA Gives Cleavage or Repression of Target mRNA
- Whither perfect or near-perfect complmentarity, miRNA can act like siRNA, giving cleavage of target
- This pathway operates extensively in plants, less so in animals

lincRNAs

* Chromosomes are arranged to allow long-range contacts between regulatory elements and promoters, gene ends, and much longer-range contacts involving multiple genes. Contacts maps have led to the view that chromosomes are arranged in a segmented way.

* Genomic methods have indicated the presence of large numbers of long, intervening non-coding RNAs (lincRNAs). While many of these RNAs are likely to be non-functional, some are known to be functional and many others are likely to be functional as well.

* Some lncRNAs act in cis on DNA adjacent to their transcription sites, while others function in trans to regulate gene expression, functioning in the nucleus or the cytoplasm.

* More functions almost certainly exist and are yet to be discovered.
